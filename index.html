Java 8 D-Day

The landing is very close, Java 8 is going to be released in just 5 days (March 18).

This is very exciting as after many years of controversy, Java will eventually have lambda expressions as part of the language. No more need to use anonymous inner classes with a single method to implement callbacks!

You can find the official list of all the new features on the OpenJdk site but other than closures, I’m also very excited about some other enhancements such as the Streams API, the new Date & Time API, and the removal of the Permgen space.

I’m going to cover some of these new features in a more detailed manner by posting dedicated posts with some code examples. But in the meantime, and if you are interested, Oracle has a Java 8 Launch Webcast with some panel discussions about some of the new features and enhancements. You can register here: http://eventreg.oracle.com/profile/web/index.cfm?PKWebId=0x637279c68

The power of abstraction

Here is an amazing talk by Barbara Liskov about how the idea of abstraction helped to address the complexity of large software projects and how it’s been gradually incorporated in modern languages.

Barbara is a Turing award winner and you might know her from the Liskov substitution principle (LSP), the L in the SOLID principles in OOP.

Barbara describes the situation and the progress made in the 60s and 70s in Programming Methodology to improve how software should be designed and structured. You probably learned in college things such as the use of modularity, the importance of avoiding Go To statements and global variables, encapsulation, etc and you might think that these were just natural things that you could take for granted.

But it actually took some time to come up with these ideas and some of them even had to overcome some controversy at that time. So it’s very interesting to learn about the history and context of these programming languages concepts that are present today in the majority of modern OOP languages.

If you like Programing Language methodology, you can not miss this talk…

Warning == Error

My first supervisor, which I consider a mentor, didn’t allow me to release a version of my code until I had zero warnings. At the time, I found it very tedious to go chase some of the warnings, specially when I knew the code was going to work fine. Come on, it was ‘just’ a warning.

But this was a very wise piece of advise, so I just wanted to transmit it now myself.

We’ll come back to the static vs dynamic language debate in the future, but if you go with a static language, you need to take advantage of the validations that are going to be made by the compiler.

A compiler can find a lot of potential problems/issues or stuff that you don’t really want to have in your code (e.g. unused variables, unreachable code, etc). Many compilers (and IDEs that include the compiler) might limit the number of warnings by default, so novice developers don’t get too scared. So it’s worth reviewing your level of warnings and increase it as much as you can.

Let me finish this post by adding a reference to the article that originated my post. It’s a must-read for anybody doing serious Objective-c development: Compiler Warnings for Objective-C Developers

And remember, in production code, a warning equals an error.

LinkedIn is going native too

A few months ago I posted about the competitiion between web and native app development and my feeling that contrary to what you would think (based on what desktop development ended up being), native app development could eventually win.

LinkedIn just released a major update of its mobile app and it seems to confirm the native apps trend. Ok, we are still far from being able to declare a winner but it’s significant that some of these big tech guys are moving to native.

VentureBeat.com has the reasons Why LinkedIn dumped HTML5 & went native for its mobile apps.

Some interesting stuff:

We have definitely shifted from HTML5 to native. The primary reason for that is, we’re seeing that more and more people are spending more time in the app, and the app is running out of memory. It’s not performance issues, like speed or rendering, but it’s still a big problem.
The second reason we’ve gone native is trying to get some of the animations — the spinners and the way they work — getting that smoothness, we felt like we needed native to really do that well.

and

There are a few things that are critically missing. One is tooling support — having a debugger that actually works, performance tools that tell you where the memory is running out.

March 23rd, 2013

The end of Google Reader and being creative

Google Reader

Big news this week, Google has decided to shut down one of these services that blew all the competitors away: Google Reader. Google stated two main reasons: the number of users is decreasing year after year and they want to focus on other products.

My take from what we’ve been seeing recently at Google is that the second reason weighed much more than the first one. I have no idea what the numbers are but from the blogosphere reaction, it’s clear that a lot of people are still using this service (me included).

We’ve seen different reactions to this news, from Om Malik announcing that he’s not going to trust Google on any of its new products anymore to Marco Arment who, as a service owner himself (Instapaper) recognizes that it’s to the company to decide if they want to continue a service or not and any service has a limited lifespan anyway.

In my opinion, the end of all these Google services opens up also a different debate. According to Google, many of these services originated from the 20% of time that the Googlers can dedicate to their own projects. It looks like since Larry Page took over, Google has been trying to kill all the services to refocus only on the products that are strategic to the company (Android, Google+ and of course Search and advertisements).

So you could think that this is going to kill the creativity of Google and could even provoke a departure of talent in the company. You know, engineers tend to be more motivated and involved when they work for something they created and designed themselves. That’s what I thought too.

And then, this week I watched the 60 Minutes show about Jack Dorsey, one of the Twitter founders,  and I noted something very interesting. He said:

(…) constraints actually help creativity, because instead of having a big blank slate that might intimidate you, having constraints make you focus.

I think this makes a lot of sense, and it’s definitely proven right with Twitter (hashtags, RT, FF, etc).

So hopefully, the end of all these products at Google will bring a lot of innovation and bright ideas into their core services.

Watch the 60 Minutes overtime video on why you can only have 140 characters per tweet.

The full 60 Minutes about Jack Dorsey is totally worth it…
