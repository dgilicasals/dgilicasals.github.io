Sketching Bits

A journey from the 8 to the 64 bits …

August 3rd, 2012

The Journey

I’ve spent most of my life sketching bits.All started when I was around 8 years old. My dad had an import business in Andorra and one of the brands he sold was Texas Instruments. At that time, in addition to the semiconductor business, Texas Instruments was very popular making pocket calculators. It turned out that Texas Instruments built a personal computer, the TI-99/4A. My dad got one for the family and I discovered the magic of computer science.From that day on, I’ve been always surrounded my computers. I played games, started programming, programmed a few simple games myself, went to college to study CS and today I’m working as a passionate computer engineer.This is a humble tribute to all these machines and to the corresponding software that I’ve been enjoying all these years.Note: I will be updating this page with additional content, from screenshots of the different systems to hopefully some of the code I wrote for each of them (if I can retrieve it).

Texas Instruments TI-99/4A

Processor TI TMS9900 (16 bits)
Clock Rate 3.0 MHz
Memory 64KB
Operating System TI Basic

This was my first computer, which surprisingly was not a 8-bit but a 16-bit machine. It had a slot for cartridges, which was the main I/O device. Since my father also sold those cartridges, mainly games, I was usually able to test them all on my machine. For free.

It came with a BASIC interpreter and its corresponding manual. So I learned to do some very rudimentary programming. Even worse, one day I found that a newspaper stand was selling a book with games to type on your machine. The funny part was that since I didn’t have any  I/O device to save the code, each time I turned off the machine, I lost all my code… Later on, I figured out that I could use a cassette player/recorder to save and load the code.

Canon V-20 MSX

Processor Zilog Z80A (8 Bits)
Clock Rate 3.25 MHz
Memory 64KB
Operating System MSX Basic


If I remember correctly,  I got my MSX for Christmas. It was another amazing machine for the time, even though it didn’t succeed in Europe the same way it did in Japan. It also had a ROM cartridge slot and some memorable games, specially the ones produced by Konami.

The other I/O was again the cassette player/recorder and it’s awful sound. Some games could take 5mns to load, so I used to put the tape to load and then come back some time later.

The machine came with a couple of manuals, one of them was a reference for BASIC. So I decided to start to code my own games.

Commodore Amiga 500

Processor Motorola 68000 (16 Bits)
Clock Rate 7.09 MHz
Memory 512KB
Operating System AmigaOS

This is the machine I dreamt about for quite some time. The MSX was getting old (they even came up with a new MSX-2 standard) and everybody was amazed by the capabilities of the two main 16-bit computers of the time: the Atari ST and the Amiga 500.

I eventually got one and it totally deserved its reputation. What an incredible machine. And no more cassette recorders anymore, it came with a 3″5 floppy drive.

As a total and passionate technology fan it was clear to me what I wanted to study: CS. My previous machine, the Commodore Amiga was still a pretty good machine but it was starting to lose some of its lead against a new wave of cheap and powerful assembled machines: the PC clones. A bunch of geeky stores opened around my college where you could buy whatever individual components you wanted to build your personalized machine.

At the same time, it was not always easy to find good and compatible compilers for the languages we learned in college for the Amiga. So I decided to jump into the PC wave.

And it didn’t take me long to discover the life beyond MS Windows thanks to the Slackware GNU/Linux distribution.
